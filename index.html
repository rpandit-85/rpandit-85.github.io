In the above dataset, data is contained for all NBA players from the 1996 season to the 2017 season. Some columns of data that could be important to our analysis are

  • Year: The year in which all statistics were recorded.
  • Tm: The team which the player played for.
  • Salary: The amount the player made, in dollars, for that particular year.
  • Pos: The position that the player was listed as filling in the roster.
  • PER: The player's Player Efficiency Rating. This metric is used to determine how effective and valuable a player is while on court.
  • WS: The amount of win shares that the player averaged throughout the season. This metric is used to determine a player's effect on winning.

There are many more columns in this dataset, with valuable information such as a player's true shooting percentage, turnovers per game, points per game, and more. This information would be very valuable if we were studying a player's effectiveness on the floor or something similar, however for the purpose of this experiment, this data is not necessary. This will be addressed once the Data is cleaned in the next step.

In this phase, you have to clean your data and make sure that it fits your needs. The first thing that I realized is that I would want to not only compare players against each other, but also teams as a whole. To achieve this purpose, I created an offshoot dataframe from player_data called team_data, created by grouping player_data by the Yr and Team columns. I then summed up all of the stats, so that it would represent the entire team. Now, I would have access to information like the total salary that a team paid all of its players for a specific season.

After this, I also realized that there was some missing data in the player_data dataset. Some of the players were listed as having a salary of 0. I'm not sure why this data is missing, but I know that keeping this data in the dataset would likely have an effect on any analysis done, and so I made sure to remove all of this data. Unfortunately, this means that this data is not 100% accurate in terms of the total salary paid by each team, but the difference is not so large that the findings should be completely disregarded.

Next, I realized that I would probably need statstics to measure how successful a team had been during a particular season. Most commonly, this is determined by a team's win-loss record. Unfortunately, the dataset that I got all my data from didn't include this information, and so I had to find it somewhere else. To get this information, I found another dataset that contained just the team's records and winning percentages, and then combined this data with the team_data dataset.

Graphing the change in NBA Salaries Over Time¶

One thing that is important to look over before any analysis is done is to see how exactly NBA salaries have changed over the multiple decades that this dataset spans, to see if there are any trends that can be spotted. Below is a scatter plot of all of the total salaries paid by each team, grouped by year, and differentiated using different colors for each individual team. A regression line has also been added to show the general trend over the years.

In [457]:
color_palette = sns.color_palette("Paired", 30)

sns.set(rc={"figure.figsize":(15, 7)})

#Creating a scatter plot of teams' salaries over time.
sns.scatterplot(x="Year", y="Salary", data=team_data, palette=color_palette, hue='Tm')
plt.legend(loc=9, bbox_to_anchor=(0.5, -0.2), ncol=5)
plt.title("Salaries over Time")
plt.xlabel("Year")
plt.ylabel("Total Team Salary")

#Adding a regression line so the trend can be determined
m, b = np.polyfit(team_data['Year'], team_data['Salary'], 1)
plt.plot(team_data['Year'], (m*team_data['Year']) + b)

plt.show()

From the plot above, the most obvious conclusion that we can draw is that there is a positive relationship between salary and time. As the years progress, the salaries that NBA teams are paying out are increasing. There could be a multitude of reasons for this, for example inflation, or the fact that the NBA has gotten more and more popular over the years, and thus has generated more revenue to be shared amongst the league. However, this could also indicate a change in mindset across the league that the more money you throw at acquiring top talent, the more you will win.

Another interesting aspect that can be seen is that for many of the years represented here, there are a few teams that are outspending the rest significantly. For example, during the last season recorded in this dataset (2016-2017), the Cleveland Cavaliers had the highest payroll in the league, and it wasn't close. Clearly, these teams had championship aspirations, and took the approach of acquiring the best talent at the highest prices. It will be interesting to look at these teams in particular further into the experiment to see if their strategy paid off.
